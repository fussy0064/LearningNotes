Module 01 — Virtual Reality

Virtual Reality

VR creates a fully synthetic, immersive digital environment that completely replaces the user's real-world surroundings. The user wears a headset that tracks head movement and renders a 360° scene in real time — creating the sensation of being physically inside another world.

Unlike watching a video on a screen, VR puts you inside the content. Your movements, direction of gaze, and position are all tracked and reflected in the virtual environment in real time.

HOW VR WORKS

01
Stereoscopic Rendering
Two slightly offset images are rendered — one per eye — creating the illusion of 3D depth, just like natural human binocular vision.
02
Head Tracking
Gyroscopes, accelerometers, and cameras track every rotation and movement of the head, updating the rendered scene instantly to maintain immersion.
03
Spatial Audio
Binaural audio makes sounds appear to come from specific directions in 3D space — footsteps from the left, voices from above — reinforcing presence.
04
Controller Interaction
Hand controllers or hand-tracking cameras allow the user to reach out and manipulate virtual objects naturally within the environment.

Module 02 — Augmented Reality

Augmented Reality

AR overlays digital content onto the real world rather than replacing it. The user continues to see their physical environment, but computer-generated images, animations, data, or 3D objects are superimposed on top — through a phone camera, smart glasses, or a mixed-reality headset.

The key distinction from VR: you never leave the real world. AR enhances your existing environment rather than constructing a new one.

HOW AR WORKS

01
Scene Capture
The device camera continuously captures the real-world environment as live video, feeding it into the AR processing pipeline.
02
World Understanding
Computer vision algorithms detect surfaces, planes, objects, lighting conditions, and spatial anchors in the real scene in real time.
03
Digital Rendering
3D objects, animations, text, or UI elements are rendered and precisely anchored to real-world positions — respecting lighting and perspective.
04
Composite Display
The real-world camera feed and rendered digital elements are composited together into a single view and shown on screen or through optical lenses.

Extended Reality — The XR Spectrum

AR and VR are not opposites — they exist on a continuum called Extended Reality (XR). Between fully physical reality and fully virtual reality sits Mixed Reality (MR), where digital and physical objects coexist and interact.

Module 04 — Core Concept

The Concept of Presence

↯ PRESENCE DEFINED
Presence is the psychological sensation of actually being somewhere or experiencing something, rather than simply viewing it on a screen. It is the defining goal of both VR and AR design — and the reason immersive technologies are fundamentally different from traditional media.

A well-designed VR experience doesn't feel like watching a film in a headset. It feels like you are physically standing in another place. When presence is achieved, users instinctively duck to avoid virtual objects flying at them, feel vertigo when standing on a virtual ledge, and form genuine emotional connections with virtual characters — because the brain responds to presence much as it does to real experience.

FACTORS THAT CREATE PRESENCE

👀
Visual Fidelity
High resolution, wide field of view, and realistic rendering reduce the perceptual gap between the virtual and real. Modern headsets target 4K per eye.
⚡
Low Latency
Tracking-to-display latency must stay below 20ms. Any perceptible lag between head movement and visual update breaks presence and causes nausea.
🎵
Spatial Audio
3D positional audio is as important as visuals for presence. Sound should convincingly originate from virtual objects and spaces.
🤲
Natural Interaction
When virtual objects respond to touch and manipulation in physically plausible ways, the brain accepts the environment as real.
